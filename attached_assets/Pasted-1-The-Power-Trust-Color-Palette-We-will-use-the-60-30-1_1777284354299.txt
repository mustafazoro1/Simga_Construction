1. The "Power & Trust" Color Palette
вЂ‹We will use the 60-30-10 Rule: 60% Primary (Navy), 30% Secondary (White/Light Gray), and 10% Accent (Construction Amber).
2. Structural Styling (Applying the Colors)
вЂ‹The "Glass" Header
вЂ‹To look classy, your header shouldn't be a solid block. Use Glassmorphism.
вЂ‹Color: #0A192F at 80% transparency.
вЂ‹Effect: Add a backdrop-filter: blur(10px).
вЂ‹Border: A 1px bottom border in #112240.
вЂ‹Result: It looks like high-end architectural glass.
вЂ‹The Hero Section (The "Hook")
вЂ‹Since you have that great fleet photo, the Hero section is your biggest selling point.
вЂ‹Overlay: Apply a gradient from left to right: rgba(10, 25, 47, 0.9) to transparent.
вЂ‹Typography: Large, bold headings in Safety Amber for the first word, and Cool White for the rest.
вЂ‹Button: Use the Safety Amber (#F59E0B) with a "Box Shadow" that glows slightly. It makes the "Call to Action" impossible to miss.
вЂ‹Service & Project Cards
вЂ‹To avoid the "flat" look, give your cards an "Elevation" effect.
вЂ‹Background: #112240.
вЂ‹Border: 1px solid #233554.
вЂ‹Hover Effect: When a user hovers, the border should turn Safety Amber and the card should lift up 5px. This feels interactive and high-quality.
вЂ‹3. Typography: The "Engineering" Font
вЂ‹Fonts carry the weight of the brand.
вЂ‹Headings: Use "Oswald" or "Montserrat". They are geometric and bold, looking like they were stamped onto steel.
вЂ‹Body Text: Use "Inter" or "Roboto". They are highly legible and look professional in technical descriptions.
вЂ‹4. The "Classy" Details
вЂ‹To push it into the "Premium" category, add these three small details:
вЂ‹Thin Gold/Amber Lines: Use very thin (1px or 2px) Amber lines to separate sections or underline headings. This mimics a "blueprint" or a "measuring tape" look.
вЂ‹Monochrome Images: In sections that aren't about specific projects (like "About Us"), use black-and-white photos with high contrast. It looks very sophisticated.
вЂ‹Dark Mode by Default: High-end engineering sites often look better in dark mode because it makes the machinery and steel in photos "pop" more than a bright white background.
